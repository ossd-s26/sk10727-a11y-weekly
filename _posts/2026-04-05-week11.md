## The Cathedral and the Bazaar

The cathedral vs bazaar was a super interesting reading, especially considering the author's background and the impact he's made. I thought his example of Popclient first solving his frustrations with local versions of emails changing domains and how it ultimately turned into Fetchmail easy to folllow and that it highlighted various important aspects of the open source process.

I also found our discussion in class very engaging, especially when talking about Netscape. I learned that the bazaar model can actually emerge out of business pressure, not just ideology. Netscape was facing intense competition from Microsoft, which bundled Internet Explorer with Windows and started dominating the browser market. As a last minute move, Netscape decided to publish its source code and open it up, which became the Mozilla project. What stood out to me is that this was not originally about open source principles, it was a strategic response to competition. Even more surprising was that Netscape was still acquired by AOL after doing this. I would have expected that making the code public would reduce its value, but that did not happen. If anything, it showed that value can come from the ecosystem and momentum around a project, not just from keeping the code private. It also helped me understand why companies today have open source parts of their work while and how they benefit from it.

<!--more-->

After our discussion, I also started thinking about how the FOSSisms change with AI.

It feels like AI makes the bazaar model even more logical becayse the barrier to contributing is much lower. It also makes it easier to test ideas, get feedback, and improve things in a more open, continuous way, which is very aligned with how the bazaar is supposed to work.

At the same time, it also makes some of the challenges more difficult. AI can generate a lot of code, but a lot of it is not perfect. So, instead of code being the main issue, the bottleneck shifts to how well the problem is structured. This connects back to the idea that smart data structures and clear design matter more than just writing a lot of code.

It also made me think more about the role of maintainers. If AI makes it easier for more people to contribute, then there is potentially even more noise, and the role of filtering and organizing contributions becomes harder. So while AI strengthens some aspects of the bazaar, it also increases the need for strong coordination and decision making.
